Methodology Reference
Calculation logic, scoring rules, and framework definitions used throughout this tool

Every KPI has a polarity — either higher is better (e.g. touchless invoice rate) or lower is better (e.g. invoice processing cost). The gap formula differs depending on polarity to ensure the gap value always represents the magnitude of underperformance.

↑ Higher is Better
Used when a higher value indicates better performance (rates, accuracy, automation coverage).
↓ Lower is Better
Used when a lower value indicates better performance (cycle times, costs, error rates).
-- HIGHER IS BETTER (e.g. Touchless Invoice Rate) Gap Value = Benchmark Target − Client Current Value Gap % = (Benchmark Target − Client Current Value) ÷ Benchmark Target -- LOWER IS BETTER (e.g. Invoice Processing Cost) Gap Value = Client Current Value − Benchmark Target Gap % = (Client Current Value − Benchmark Target) ÷ |Benchmark Target| -- INTERPRETATION Positive Gap % → Client is underperforming vs target Zero or Negative Gap % → Client meets or exceeds target
Worked Example — Touchless Invoice Rate (P2P)
Polarity: Higher is better  |  Unit: %
Benchmark Target (Upper Quartile): 45%  |  Client Current Value: 22%

Gap Value = 45 − 22 = 23 percentage points
Gap % = (45 − 22) ÷ 45 = 23 ÷ 45 = 51.1%

Interpretation: The client is 51.1% below the upper-quartile benchmark — this maps to Critical severity.
ℹ If no client value is entered, gap and severity display as N/A. The benchmark target used in the calculation is determined by the Target Selection logic (see section 5).

Once Gap % is calculated, it is bucketed into one of five severity levels. These thresholds are applied consistently across all KPIs regardless of sub-function, unit, or polarity.

SeverityGap % RangeInterpretationTypical Action
On Track≤ 5%Performing at or near benchmark. Minor variance within noise range.Monitor; sustain controls and process discipline.
Low> 5% to 15%Small but real gap. Improvement possible with targeted focus.Targeted fixes; process ownership; data quality review.
Medium> 15% to 30%Meaningful underperformance. Likely process or adoption gap.Root-cause analysis; workflow remediation; selective automation.
High> 30% to 50%Significant gap. Structural process or control deficiency likely.Focused improvement initiative; governance escalation.
Critical> 50%Major gap versus benchmark. Transformation priority.Executive-sponsored programme; process redesign; technology enablement.
-- SEVERITY BUCKETING LOGIC if gap_pct ≤ 0.05 → "On Track" elif gap_pct ≤ 0.15 → "Low" elif gap_pct ≤ 0.30 → "Medium" elif gap_pct ≤ 0.50 → "High" else → "Critical"
Worked Example — Touchless Invoice Rate
Gap % = 51.1%  →  51.1% > 50%  →  Severity = Critical

Second example — On-time Payment Rate: Gap % = 7.3%  →  5% < 7.3% ≤ 15%  →  Severity = Low

The Priority Score combines three dimensions to rank KPIs by transformation urgency. A KPI with a large gap but low strategic importance ranks lower than a KPI with a moderate gap in a high-impact, automatable area. Scores are expressed on a 0–100 scale for readability.

-- PRIORITY SCORE FORMULA Priority Score = (0.45 × Normalised Gap Score) + (0.30 × Business Impact ÷ 5) + (0.25 × Automation Potential ÷ 5) -- NORMALISED GAP SCORE Normalised Gap = min(gap_pct, 1.0) ← capped at 100% -- DISPLAYED AS Display Score = Priority Score × 100 ← rounded to integer
ComponentWeightRangeRationale
Normalised Gap Score45%0.0 – 1.0Largest driver — gaps that are further from benchmark warrant more attention.
Business Impact30%1 – 5 scaleHow significant is this KPI to financial outcomes, controls, or stakeholder trust?
Automation Potential25%1 – 5 scaleCan this gap be meaningfully closed through automation or technology enablement?
Worked Example — Touchless Invoice Rate
Gap % = 51.1% → Normalised Gap = 0.511
Business Impact = 4 / 5 = 0.80
Automation Potential = 5 / 5 = 1.00

Priority Score = (0.45 × 0.511) + (0.30 × 0.80) + (0.25 × 1.00)
               = 0.230 + 0.240 + 0.250 = 0.720
Display Score = 0.720 × 100 = 72 / 100
ℹ Business Impact and Automation Potential scores (1–5) are editable per KPI in the KPI Library tab, allowing facilitators to calibrate scoring to client context before or during a workshop.

Each KPI carries four benchmark reference points derived from industry research, practitioner synthesis, and sources including APQC and Hackett Group data. These represent the performance distribution across finance organisations of comparable scale and complexity.

TierPositionDescriptionTypical Profile
Laggard Bottom 25% Performance at or below the 25th percentile. Significant process, technology or governance deficiency. Heavily manual, fragmented, reactive finance function.
Median 50th percentile Typical finance organisation performance. Partial digitisation, some automation, functional controls. Mixed legacy and modern systems, inconsistent process adoption.
Upper Quartile 75th percentile High-performing finance organisation. Strong process discipline, broad automation, good data quality. Standardised processes, ERP discipline, active continuous improvement culture.
World Class Top 5–10% Best-in-class performance. Near-full automation, AI-enabled, real-time visibility, minimal exceptions. Fully digitised, predictive analytics, continuous close, AI-enabled FP&A.
Worked Example — Touchless Invoice Rate Benchmark Range
Laggard: 10%  |  Median: 25%  |  Upper Quartile: 45%  |  World Class: 65%

A client at 22% sits between Laggard and Median — above laggard but materially below median. This reflects partial automation adoption with significant room to improve through workflow standardisation and OCR/AI-based invoice processing.
ℹ Benchmark values are sourced from APQC, Hackett Group data, and practitioner synthesis. They represent directional guidance rather than statistically precise population distributions, and should be contextualised to the client's industry, scale, and ERP landscape.

Business Impact measures how much closing this KPI gap would matter to financial outcomes, stakeholder trust, risk exposure, or operational effectiveness. It is not a measure of how hard the gap is to close — only of how consequential it is if left unaddressed. Score this from the perspective of: "If we fixed this gap, how significantly would it benefit the organisation?"

ScoreLevelWhat it means in a finance contextExample KPI types
1

2

3

4

Risk exposure if not addressed. DSO, collection effectiveness, cash forecast accuracy, tax provision cycle time, month-end close cycle
5
 Critical Directly affects financial integrity, regulatory compliance, audit outcomes, or investor/board confidence. Duplicate payment rate, bad debt write-off, audit adjustments, unauthorized change incidents, ETR variance to plan

Scoring Guide — Quick Decision Rules
Score 1 if the fix is primarily about process discipline, master data, or ERP config — not new tooling.
Score 2 if structured workflow or rule-based bots would solve the majority of the problem.
Score 3 if OCR, intelligent document processing, or ML-based matching is the primary lever.
Score 4 if AI-powered forecasting, predictive analytics, or modern EPM capabilities are the key enabler.
Score 5 if GenAI, LLM-driven automation, or agentic process handling represents the transformation horizon.
ℹ Scores of 4–5 do not imply the client is ready for that technology today — they indicate that the ceiling of value for this KPI sits in AI/GenAI territory. The score influences priority ranking: high automation potential combined with a large gap signals a high-return investment opportunity.

Every KPI in the tracker is tagged with a polarity that defines what "good" looks like. Polarity is used in gap calculation, slider direction, and insight generation. Assigning polarity correctly when creating custom KPIs is essential for accurate scoring.

↑ Higher is Better
Definition: A higher value indicates stronger performance. The client wants to maximise this metric.

Gap formula: Gap = Target − Client Value
A positive gap means the client is below target — underperforming.

Finance examples: Touchless invoice rate, forecast accuracy, on-time payment rate, cash application auto-match rate, collection effectiveness index, self-service dashboard adoption
↓ Lower is Better
Definition: A lower value indicates stronger performance. The client wants to minimise this metric.

Gap formula: Gap = Client Value − Target
A positive gap means the client is above target — underperforming.

Finance examples: Invoice processing cost, days sales outstanding, month-end close cycle time, manual journal rate, duplicate payment rate, tax provision cycle time
How to decide polarity for a custom KPI
Ask: "Would a finance leader prefer this number to go up or down over time?"

If the answer is up (more automation, higher accuracy, better coverage) → Higher is better
If the answer is down (fewer errors, shorter cycle times, lower cost) → Lower is better

Watch out for: Metrics that could go either way depending on context (e.g. "number of exceptions" is almost always lower is better even if exceptions can sometimes be positive signals).

The Priority Score is a composite index designed to surface the KPIs most deserving of transformation investment — those with large gaps, high strategic stakes, and strong technology-enabled closure potential. Understanding each component helps facilitators defend the ranking in workshop conversations.

-- PRIORITY SCORE FORMULA (recap) Priority Score = (0.45 × Normalised Gap) + (0.30 × Business Impact ÷ 5) + (0.25 × Automation Potential ÷ 5) Display Score = Priority Score × 100 ← 0 to 100 scale
ComponentWeightWhat it capturesWhy this weightRange in formula
Normalised Gap
45%
 How far the client is from the target benchmark, normalised to 0–1 (capped at 100% gap). The core signal of underperformance. Gap magnitude is the primary driver of urgency. A KPI far from benchmark demands attention regardless of other factors. Highest weight reflects this primacy. 0.00 – 1.00
Business Impact
30%
 The strategic and financial consequence of leaving the gap unaddressed (1–5 scale, see section 9). Converts to 0.2–1.0 in formula. Impact ensures that a small gap in a critical KPI (e.g. duplicate payments) outranks a large gap in a low-consequence one. Anchors priority in business value, not just size. 0.20 – 1.00
Automation Potential
25%
 The degree to which this gap can be closed through technology enablement (1–5 scale, see section 10). Converts to 0.2–1.0 in formula. Automation potential reflects return on investment — a gap that can be closed through automation delivers sustainable, scalable improvement. Lower weight than impact because technology readiness is secondary to strategic urgency. 0.20 – 1.00
Interpreting the display score
70–100: Top transformation priorities — large gap, high strategic stakes, strong automation potential. These should anchor Wave 1.
50–69: Important improvement areas — meaningful gap with real business consequence. Include in Wave 1 or early Wave 2.
30–49: Medium priority — moderate gap or lower impact. Wave 2 candidates or operational improvement items.
Below 30: Monitor only — small gap, limited consequence, or limited automation leverage. Review periodically but do not consume transformation capacity.
ℹ The weights (45/30/25) are calibrated defaults reflecting typical transformation programme priorities. For clients with specific contexts — e.g. a compliance-focused organisation where impact should dominate, or a technology-led transformation where automation potential matters more — facilitators can adjust Business Impact and Automation Potential scores per KPI to shift the ranking accordingly.
